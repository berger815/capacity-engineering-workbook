📘 Capacity & Engineering Workbook — Operating Guide

Use this guide to build a defensible capacity model, understand how each calculation works, verify that the inputs are complete, and translate the result into an executable recovery plan. The workbook remains one self-contained browser file; working data stays in this browser until exported.

What LT Adjust changes

With LT Adjust OFF, the 252 required hours remain at June delivery timing. With LT Adjust ON and Spread OFF, all 252 hours move to the Testing phase start-by month. With Spread ON, the same 252 hours are distributed across the true 4→0 week phase window. A calendar boundary may place portions in one or two months, but the total remains 252 hours.

Footprint calculation

Average WIP = 120 units × 5 dwell days ÷ 20 working days/month = 30 units
Peak WIP = 30 × 1.2 = 36 units
Required area = 36 × 3 sq ft/unit = 108 sq ft
Interpretation: Capacity load follows the department’s phase timing when LT Adjust is enabled. Footprint intentionally uses ship-date monthly demand and dwell. These are related but different planning questions.

Valid phase rules

  • Start Week must be greater than End Week.
  • Week values must be between 0 and 260.
  • Every department must be assigned to exactly one valid phase for every product line.
  • Deleting a referenced phase creates an orphaned assignment that must be corrected.
Forecast boundary: Work shifted before the first forecast month cannot be displayed. Capacity Analysis reports clipped hours. Add an earlier buffer year when the longest lead time approaches the horizon boundary.
Four-week phase: Four weeks is not the same as one named calendar month. Depending on the delivery boundary, a 4→0 week phase can touch one or two months. Spread conserves total hours.

Required load

Required Hours = Demand × Standard Hours × (1 + Rework%) × Office Copy Factor

Copy Factor applies only to Office departments. A 50% factor means only half the product demand generates that office activity.

Available capacity

Theoretical = Base Hours × Efficiency%
OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality
Demonstrated = entered annual demonstrated hours

Demonstrated capacity overrides theoretical capacity when populated and is marked [D]. Otherwise the model uses [T].

Manual efficiency

If OEE is not entered, usable efficiency equals 100% minus Holiday/Vacation, Planned Downtime, Unplanned Downtime, and Training. Rework increases required hours; it does not reduce available hours.

Do not trust a green cell by itself. Zero demand, missing standard hours, or missing capacity can also produce an apparently harmless result. Confirm the source badges and completeness first.

Equipment load

Machine Load = Demand × Machine Hours/Unit × (1 + Department Rework%)

Machine routing is independent from labor standard hours. A product may require labor time and machine time simultaneously, so the two constraints are never added together.

Equipment capacity

Net Machine Hours = Installed Quantity × Scheduled Hours/Machine/Year × Equipment OEE%

Equipment OEE is specific to that machine group. Department labor efficiency is not applied to machine capacity.

Three-part workflow

Define installed assets and machine-hour routing on Machines & Equipment. Review utilization, capacity balance, and the governing constraint in Capacity Analysis. Enter planned machines, scheduled hours, planned OEE, optional outsourced load relief, and the in-service date on Scenarios & Recovery. Before that month, current capacity applies; from that month forward, the plan values apply.

  • Use one equipment group for resources that share the same routing and available-hour basis.
  • Separate unlike assets—for example, 3-axis mills and 5-axis mills—when products consume them differently.
  • Machine load follows the assigned department’s lead-time phase and Spread setting.
  • The governing constraint compares labor utilization with each equipment group; the highest valid utilization governs.
  • A machine with routed demand but zero capacity is reported as No Capacity, not as 0% utilization.
  • Installed-machine square feet is shown separately from WIP footprint to prevent accidental double counting.
Operator coupling: equipment capacity does not prove labor is available to run it. Review both the machine result and its department labor result.
Average WIP = Monthly Ship Demand × Dwell Working Days ÷ Working Days/Month
Peak WIP = Average WIP × Monthly Peak Factor
Required Space = Peak WIP × Space per Unit
  • Production departments use square feet and Sq Ft/Unit.
  • Warehouse departments default to cubic feet and Cubic Ft/Unit; each warehouse can instead use pallet positions.
  • Square feet, cubic feet, and pallet positions are never added together.
  • The department peak is the highest simultaneous monthly product mix—not the sum of each product’s unrelated peak.
  • The combined production KPI uses the highest month across production departments—not the sum of independent departmental maxima.
  • Annual-only demand falls evenly across months and therefore reproduces the original annual-average estimate.
Required inputs: every product with demand needs Dwell Days and Space/Unit for each applicable production or warehouse department. Available space is also required to calculate the gap.
Required Headcount = Ceiling(Period Load Hours ÷ (Period Hours/FTE × Target Utilization))
Headcount Gap = Required Headcount − Current FTE
  • Use realistic productive hours/FTE/year; do not substitute paid hours without accounting for losses.
  • Target utilization creates operating margin. Lower targets increase the modeled staffing requirement.
  • Hire By subtracts the hiring lead time from the first period with a positive headcount gap.
  • LT Adjust may move the staffing need earlier because personnel must be present when the department work begins.

Recovery planning

For labor, select a capacity scenario and add the trigger date, owner, investment, and dated milestones. Milestone FTE or capacity adders build the labor recovery curve. For equipment, select the machine group and enter additions, planned hours, planned OEE, relief, and its in-service date. The Action Log should capture specific decisions, owners, due dates, priorities, and closure status.

Good recovery plan: identifies the constraint, quantifies the gap, states the countermeasure, includes lead time and investment, assigns one owner, and shows when recovered capacity becomes usable.

⏳ Lead Times — Phase Configuration v6.82

Define independent lead time phases per MLI. Assign each department to exactly one phase per MLI. The model places full demand at Delivery Date − Phase Start Week for that department's capacity calculation.
Complete Setup and generate the workbook first.
How it works: Each MLI has its own phases and every department is assigned to one phase. With Spread OFF, the full phase load is placed at the Start Week before delivery. With Spread ON, hours are distributed from Start Week through End Week. Ship-date demand is never modified. Invalid or missing assignments keep demand at delivery timing and raise an error.
